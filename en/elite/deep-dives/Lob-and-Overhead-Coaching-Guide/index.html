    Lob and Overhead: The Vertical Game¶

    Watch any point at your club that ends in four shots or fewer. Watch the geometry of it. The shot that actually ends almost never a flat, cross-court groundstroke — it's almost always a shot that went up or down. A high lob the opponent can't reach. A smash they can only block.

    Recreational tennis gets dominated by the horizontal game — cross-court, down-the-line, angled shots hunting for space. But the vertical game — lobs and overheads — is what actually decides points, especially once your opponents are good enough to chase down anything you hit side to side. This is about that vertical game: two shots, really one pair of answers to the same problem. The lob is you hitting the ball up. The overhead is them hitting it up, and you putting it down.

    Here's a way to think about it: horizontal shots gain you space. Vertical shots change the rules of the point entirely. A lob doesn't just land in the corner — it tells your opponent, you now have to hit the next shot from below your shoelaces, head craned back, standing still or moving backward. That's a completely different problem than returning a flat ball while balanced at the net. That's exactly why lobs and overheads decide so many points.

    Most 3.5 players think of the lob and the overhead as separate strokes. They're not — they're paired answers to the same situation: the ball is high, or your opponent is at net. There are really only four situations that call for vertical tennis. You're at the baseline, your opponent's at net, and you're under pressure — that's your defensive slice lob. You're at the baseline, your opponent's at the baseline, and you want to attack — that's the offensive topspin lob, a shot for later. Your opponent hits you a high ball — that's your overhead. You're at net and they lob over you — that's either an overhead from the net, or, if you can't get back in time, a lob volley as your emergency parachute.

    Here's the trap almost every 3.5 player falls into: they only ever practice the last one — the defensive lob volley at net — and never touch the defensive lob off the baseline or the overhead itself. So they panic on every high ball. They hit a weak, floaty "moon" lob when they needed a deep slice. They flail at overheads instead of finishing points. The whole point of working through this is simple: practice all four situations, and start with the two calmest ones — the defensive lob and the controlled overhead.

    Worth knowing too: the lob and the overhead are the two most joint-friendly shots in tennis. The lob is a slow, low-impact motion with no shoulder load and no violent rotation. The overhead uses gravity as its engine — you're not swinging hard, you're just pointing the racket at the ball and letting it fall. Both shots are actually gentler on your body than a baseline rally. That's exactly why we're leading with them.

    The Lob: Your Insurance Policy¶

    The lob might be the most underappreciated shot in 3.5 tennis — not because it isn't flashy, but because it flat-out works. A 50+ player who can hit a deep, arcing defensive lob into the opponent's weaker corner will save more points over a season than a player who can crack a 70 mph forehand. The lob is slow on your body, brutal on theirs, and asks nothing at all of your shoulder.

    There are two lobs, and you should lead with the first. The defensive slice lob is a high, deep, slow arc with underspin, hit off the Continental grip — your volley grip — when you're under pressure at the baseline and your opponent's up at net. The offensive topspin lob is faster and lower, dipping back down after clearing the net, hit off your normal groundstroke grip when you're attacking a baseline opponent who's floating you weak, loopy balls.

    Lead with the slice. The topspin version looks gorgeous on TV, but at a 3.5 level it demands four things you have to get right simultaneously — reading your opponent's depth, loading your legs, generating real racket speed, and hitting a razor-thin margin. Miss any one of the four and the ball's in the bottom of the net. The defensive slice lob asks for exactly three things: Continental grip, a high finish, and a deep aim. That's it. Think of the defensive lob as your insurance policy, and the offensive lob as something for the brave, once the insurance policy is second nature.

    Setting up the defensive lob. Grip is Continental — the same V-to-bevel-2 grip you already use for the volley, and it's the single most important decision here. An Eastern or Semi-Western grip forces the face open at contact and sends the ball long. Your stance should be open or semi-open, front foot already angled slightly toward the sideline rather than square to the net — this lets your chest rotate upward into the shot instead of into the ball. And the backswing needs to be short and high: the racket goes back like a serve's takeback, head up, elbow bent, almost like you're about to scratch your own back with the racket. That high backswing is what actually gives the lob its arc — a low backswing gives you a flat shot, which is just an overhead you're serving up to your opponent.

    A good way to feel this: stand in ready position and lift the racket as if to scratch the middle of your back. That's your lob backswing. The higher it goes, the higher your arc goes — and that's a good thing. Most 3.5 players end up hitting a weak "moon" lob because they keep the backswing low and try to lift the ball with their wrist instead. Don't lift with the wrist. Lift with the backswing.

    Making contact. You want to catch the ball in front of your body, slightly higher than a normal groundstroke contact — chest-to-shoulder height on the forehand side, a touch lower, waist-to-chest, on the backhand. The racket face should sit slightly open, which the Continental grip gives you naturally — think of it as holding a little water in the strings. A flat face gives you a flat shot, and a flat shot is a free smash for your opponent. Your swing path runs low to high, brushing up the back of the ball, more vertical than a normal groundstroke — imagine drawing a line straight up from your knee to your head, and that's roughly your swing path. Right at contact, shrug your hitting shoulder up toward your ear — that small motion adds six to twelve inches of extra height to the arc, and because it's gentle rather than violent, it doesn't strain the shoulder at all.

    Here's a way to check it in the mirror: get into your open stance, do the scratch-your-back backswing, imagine the ball at chest height in front of you, then sweep the racket from low at the knee up to high at the head, face slightly open, shoulder shrugging at the top. Your racket should finish above your head — almost a mini-serve. Do ten air swings like that before you ever add a ball.

    Aiming it. You want the lob landing within two to three feet of the baseline, near the deuce or ad sideline. Land it in the middle of the service box and you've handed your opponent an easy overhead; land it in the corner and you've forced them into a 20-foot sprint or a desperately off-balance smash. There's a useful floor and ceiling here too: the minimum arc clears your opponent's highest reach by about two feet, and the maximum useful arc just clears the net by three to four feet — anything higher than that just burns time and gives your opponent a chance to reset. And if you know your opponent's backhand overhead is shaky, aim there specifically — the lob's job isn't just to clear them, it's to force the hardest possible overhead.

    A quick test before you commit to the shot: will this ball clear the highest point of their reach, and land within about three feet of the baseline? If both answers are yes, hit it. If either is no, add more shoulder shrug for arc, or aim deeper into the court. A lob that fails that test is just a free point for your opponent.

    The offensive topspin lob, briefly — this one's the inverse of the defensive lob, same basic idea, totally different execution. Use your regular groundstroke grip, since the face needs to close over the top of the ball to generate topspin. Contact is lower and more aggressive, around waist height, brushing up the back of the ball from about 7 o'clock to 1 o'clock (5 to 11 for a backhand). The trajectory is a lower arc that lands inside the court and kicks up after the bounce — which is exactly what makes it uncomfortable for your opponent. Use it against a baseline player hitting weak, loopy balls; it's a genuine counter-attack, not a defensive shot. The trap most players fall into here is trying to hit it with a Continental grip, which just produces a slice lob instead of a topspin one — you need the groundstroke grip and a genuinely faster swing, since this is a real groundstroke that happens to go up.

    At 50+, the lob is your best friend precisely because it's the lowest-stress, highest-percentage shot in the sport — no shoulder load, no violent rotation, no sprinting, no sudden stops. Just grip, backswing, lift, aim deep. Give the defensive slice lob two solid months of practice before you even think about the offensive version. Your shoulder — and your match record — will both thank you.

    The Overhead: The Sane Smash¶

    The overhead might be the single most under-hit shot in 3.5 tennis. Recreational players see a high ball, panic, spin in circles trying to get under it, and either shank it into the fence or float it back as a weak moonball. Here's the truth that changes everything: an overhead is just a serve that comes to you. Same grip, Continental. Same contact point, racket head above the ball, full extension. Same finish, over your non-dominant shoulder. The only difference is the ball's already in the air instead of being tossed. Once you see the overhead as a serve that's already happened, the panic disappears — you have all the time in the world, and you don't need to swing hard. You just need to point the racket at the ball and let gravity do the rest.

    Why recreational players actually miss overheads comes down to four things. They don't get into position early enough — the ball's rising, they're still at the baseline, and by the time they've circled underneath it, it's already at its peak; you need to be set before it peaks, which means tracking the lob from the instant it leaves your opponent's strings. They look up at the sky — the moment your head tilts back, your whole body starts leaning back with it, taking your legs out of the shot even though your legs are the actual engine here; keep your head still, eyes on the ball, chin level. They try to swing the overhead like a groundstroke — a groundstroke generates pace by swinging, but an overhead generates pace by falling, the racket dropping from a high position with gravity doing the work, so no real swing is needed, just point and fall. And they use the wrong grip — Eastern or Semi-Western opens the face too much at contact and the ball sails long; Continental is mandatory here, exactly like the serve.

    Treat every overhead as a serve your opponent just tossed for you. You have all the time in the world to set up, the ball's coming down at a comfortable pace, the net's the same height, the court's the same shape — the only real difference from your own serve is that you're intercepting instead of tossing. Make that mental shift and your success rate on overheads tends to jump dramatically.

    The tracking and setup. The instant your opponent makes contact with the lob, side-shuffle so the ball will land one to two feet in front of your lead foot — that's the sweet spot. Turn sideways so your non-dominant shoulder points at the ball; you're now in a serve-ready position. Plant both feet with knees slightly bent — front foot at roughly 45 degrees to the net, back foot parallel, weight settled 60/40 toward the back foot. And lift both arms into the trophy position: your non-dominant hand points up at the ball, tracking it the way it would track a toss, while your racket arm lifts with the head above your head and the elbow bent. If you've ever hit a serve, your arms should look identical here — the overhead really is just the next rep of the same motion.

    The contact and finish. Reach up to full extension, racket head above the ball, catching it at the highest comfortable point of your reach. The face stays slightly closed, moving down and through the ball — the Continental grip puts you there naturally, and the downward path plus gravity is what sends the ball down into the court. A useful swap for your inner monologue: instead of "smash it," think "put it down." You're not generating the pace — gravity already is. You're just placing the ball. Aim cross-court as your default, since it gives you more net clearance and more margin; save down-the-line for when you have a truly clean look at it. Finish over your non-dominant shoulder with the racket head pointing down and back, same as a serve — if you finish over your dominant shoulder or out in front of your body, your chest didn't actually turn through the shot.

    Watch a pro hit an overhead sometime: the racket head starts high, the ball's below it, and the racket simply falls through the ball. The ball's in the air for less than half a second — there's no time to swing, only time to point and let go. What looks like a "swing" is really just the racket continuing its downward path after contact's already happened. Less is genuinely more here.

    The trap most recreational players fall into is trying to muscle the overhead — gripping tight, swinging hard, and watching the ball sail long or bury itself in the net. The sane version is the opposite: grip pressure around 3-4 out of 10, swing speed at maybe 30% of your forehand, letting gravity and the downward angle do the actual work. A controlled 50 mph overhead wins the point at 3.5. A panicked 80 mph swing is just a fault.

    There are three situations worth knowing the adjustment for. An easy high ball, one your opponent's lob left short, calls for the standard version — full extension, aim cross-court, place it. A deep lob that lands behind you calls for turning and shuffling back until the ball's in front of you again — don't try to smash something that's behind your body, since that's a backhand smash, and honestly the easier answer is usually to just let a too-deep lob bounce and hit a normal groundstroke instead. And if you're still moving backward as the lob comes down, plant your feet first and hit second — hitting on the run is how errors happen, since the sane smash is a still shot, not a moving one.

    At 50+, this shot is your reward for hitting good defensive lobs earlier in the point — the ball comes to you, gravity does the heavy lifting, and your shoulder never takes a real load. The one thing to watch is your neck — it doesn't love craning backward. Keep your chin level, track the ball with your eyes, and let it come down to you rather than reaching up for it. If a lob is so high you'd have to crane your neck to track it, let it bounce. A controlled 3.5 groundstroke off the bounce beats an overstretched overhead every time.

    The Specialty Shots¶

    The lob volley and the counter-overhead round out your toolkit — situational shots you won't use every point, but which are genuinely devastating when the moment calls for them.

    The lob volley is your net emergency parachute. Use it when you're at the net, your opponent's at the net or closing in, and you simply can't get a ball past them — it's the "I can't pass you, so I'll go over you" shot. Grip stays Continental, same as the defensive lob. Contact needs to be high, at or above shoulder height — this shot only works from a high strike; anything at waist height or lower should be a normal volley or half-volley instead, since a lob volley off a low ball is basically a guaranteed net error. Open the face almost to vertical and lift the ball, with your non-dominant hand reaching up to help track it and balance your shoulders. Aim the same way you would a groundstroke lob — deep, into the corner; a short lob volley is just handing your opponent a smash. And the moment it's in the air, recover forward to the net rather than retreating — if your opponent tracks it down, you want to already be in position for the next shot.

    Think of the lob volley as a parachute, not a winner — it doesn't end the point, it gets you a soft landing out of a dangerous spot so you can re-attack on the very next shot. If you've already got the defensive lob down, this is really the same shot, just thirty feet closer to the net.

    The counter-overhead is your net finishing move. Use it when you're at the net, your opponent lobs over you, and you actually have time to set up. It's mechanically identical to the sane smash from Chapter 3 — same grip, same trophy position, same fall-not-swing contact — the only real difference is you have less time, since you're closer to the net and the ball has less distance to fall. That means you need to start tracking it earlier: the instant the lob leaves your opponent's racket, side-shuffle back before it even reaches its peak, turn into the serve-ready stance once you're in position, and then hit the same sane smash. The trap here is panicking and sprinting straight back without ever turning your body — players arrive at the ball looking straight up, off-balance, and float a weak reply. The fix is simply: track it, shuffle back, then turn.

    Worth knowing for doubles: the vertical game might be the single best weapon a 50+ player has there. Net opponents in doubles are usually only eight to ten feet away from you, and a defensive lob is the easiest way to neutralize them — they're forced into a high overhead while on the move, which your partner can often poach outright. Partner serves, you hit a deep defensive lob, partner poaches the weak reply — three shots, one point. That's about as good a cheat code as 50+ doubles offers.

    Drills You Can Start This Week¶

    Drill 1 — Scratch Your Back Air Swing. Solo, two minutes a day. Get into your open stance with a Continental grip, do the scratch-your-back backswing, sweep low to high with the face slightly open and a shoulder shrug at the top. Twenty air swings a day for two weeks. The backswing is exactly what most 3.5 players skip — they go straight from ready position to hit — but the backswing is the shot. Without it, there's no arc.

    Drill 2 — Sane Smash Trophy Position. Solo, three minutes a day. Racket in Continental grip, ball in your free hand, lift both arms into the trophy position — non-dominant arm pointing at an imaginary ball, racket arm bent with the head above. Hold for ten seconds, ten reps. This builds the body memory that the serve and overhead trophy positions are identical — if you can already hold it for the serve, you can hold it here too.

    Drill 3 — Sane Smash from a Tossed Ball. Solo or with a partner, ten minutes. Stand at the baseline in serve stance while a partner at the service line tosses the ball high toward your forehand overhead. Track it, get into position, turn, hit the sane smash. Goal: ten in a row landing in the correct service box. If your racket feels like it's swinging through the ball rather than falling through it, you're trying too hard.

    Drill 4 — Defensive Lob Rally. With a partner, ten minutes. Feed an easy ball to your partner, they hit a deep groundstroke back to you, you answer with a defensive slice lob, they let it bounce and return it, you lob again. Goal: five consecutive exchanges with no error. If your lob keeps landing in the service box, your arc's too low or your aim too short — add more shoulder shrug. If it's landing past the baseline, you're overhitting — dial back the shrug and let the backswing carry it instead.

    Drill 5 — Lob Volley Triangle. With a partner, five minutes. Partner at the baseline, you at net on the ad side, imagining the deuce corner as your target. Partner feeds a ball at your feet or body, you answer with a lob volley to that deuce corner. Ten reps, then switch sides. If it keeps going into the net, you're catching the ball too low — let it rise to shoulder height before you hit it.

    Seven Common Errors Worth Fixing¶

    A flat lob usually means your backswing was too low and your path too horizontal — the ball flies low and gets smashed straight back. Fix it with a higher backswing, a low-to-high path, and the shoulder shrug. A lob hit with the wrong grip — Semi-Western or Eastern — sails long; Continental is mandatory for the defensive lob, and the groundstroke grip is only for the offensive topspin version. A short lob that lands in the service box just hands your opponent an easy overhead; aim three feet short of the baseline, into the corner, instead. A panicked overhead — running at the ball and swinging hard — sends it long or into the net; the fix is the full sane smash sequence, track-position-turn-fall, with grip at 3-4/10 and swing at maybe 30%. Craning the neck to look up at the ball throws off your whole balance; keep your head still and chin level, and let the ball come down to you. Trying to hit an overhead on a ball that's drifted behind your body is a losing bet — if the lob's too deep, let it bounce and hit a normal groundstroke instead. And attempting a lob volley from a low contact point, say waist height, is basically a guaranteed net error — that shot only works from shoulder height or above; anything lower should be a regular volley or half-volley.

    The 50+ Reality Check¶

    A few honest notes on how this shot pair treats an older body, and why it's actually one of the kinder parts of the game. Shoulder strain on the overhead usually comes from cranking the arm on a high ball; the sane smash sidesteps that entirely, since gravity's doing the work and grip pressure never needs to climb above 3-4/10 — reach for the ball, don't crank at it. Neck strain comes from craning backward to track a high ball; keep your head still, let the ball come down to you, and if it's too high to track comfortably, just let it bounce. Knee strain shows up when players sprint back into position after hitting a lob; use small-step recovery instead — the whole point of a lob is that it buys you time, so use that time to walk back rather than sprint. Back strain on the overhead usually means hyperextending the lower back to reach a high ball; bend the knees instead, using the trophy position's natural knee bend and hip hinge rather than arching your spine. Wrist and elbow strain on the offensive lob comes from brushing too hard for topspin; honestly, at 50+ it's fine to skip the offensive lob entirely, since the defensive slice lob does the same job with zero strain. A loss of confidence on high balls, where fear turns your overhead into a weak floating "moon" ball, gets rebuilt fastest through Drill 3 — ten sane smashes in a row genuinely rewires the instinct. And if you're only ever playing horizontal tennis, letting points get decided purely by groundstroke exchanges, lead with the defensive lob once a session; within a month it tends to become reflex.

    The single most important thing to remember about both shots: you're not trying to win the point with a lob or an overhead. You're trying to not lose it. A defensive lob that lands deep still buys you recovery time even if your opponent retrieves it. A sane smash that lands in the right service box still puts you in attack position even if they get it back. Survival first, placement second, pace dead last — always.

    One-Page Cheat Sheet¶

    LOB & OVERHEAD — QUICK REFERENCE

THE BIG IDEA
Tennis is a vertical game, not just a horizontal one.
The lob buys you time. The overhead cashes the time in.

THE FOUR VERTICAL SITUATIONS
1. Under pressure at baseline, opponent at net → Defensive lob (slice)
2. At baseline, attacking a weak loopy ball → Offensive lob (topspin) — for the brave
3. Opponent hits you a high ball → Sane Smash — track, position, turn, fall
4. You're at net and can't pass → Lob volley, or counter-overhead if there's time

THE DEFENSIVE LOB
Grip: Continental
Stance: Open or semi-open
Backswing: High ("scratch your back")
Contact: In front, chest-to-shoulder height
Swing path: Low to high, with a shoulder shrug
Aim: 3 feet inside the baseline, into the corner

THE SANE SMASH
Grip: Continental
Tracking: Side-shuffle so the ball lands 1-2 ft in front of the lead foot
Stance: Serve-ready, trophy position, both arms up
Contact: Full extension, racket falling down through the ball
Grip pressure: 3-4/10 — "fall, don't swing"
Aim: Cross-court by default
Rule: "Put it down," not "smash it"

THE LOB VOLLEY
Grip: Continental
Contact: High — shoulder height or above, never lower
Face: Open, almost vertical
Aim: Deep, into the corner
Recovery: Forward to the net, never retreat

THE 50+ RULES
- Sane Smash: gravity does the work, no muscle needed
- The lob is your insurance policy — no shoulder load, no sprint
- Let a too-high or too-deep lob bounce rather than overstretching
- Survival first, then placement, then pace — always

FIVE CUES TO REMEMBER
1. "Scratch your back" — high backswing, high arc
2. "Fall, don't swing" — gravity, not muscle
3. "Put it down" — placement over pace
4. "Aim deep, into the corner" — the lob's only real target
5. "Survive, then shine" — don't lose the point before you try to win it

    Start this week with Drill 1 — the scratch-your-back air swing, twenty reps a day — and Drill 3, the sane smash off a tossed ball. Give it two weeks and the lob starts to feel light, almost effortless, while the sane smash quietly replaces the old panic. The lob buys you time. The overhead cashes it in.
