    Doubles Tactics: Communication, Formations & The 50+ Longevity Game¶

    A guide for 3.5-level recreational players — simple cues, clear drills, and an honest look at what changes after 50.

    The Big Idea: Doubles Is a Different Sport¶

    Go watch any 50+ recreational tournament. The singles draw has eight players in it. The doubles draw has thirty-two. That's not a coincidence — doubles is where 50+ players go to keep playing tennis for another twenty years.

    There are three reasons for that. You cover half the court instead of all of it. Points end faster, because volleys finish things that would otherwise turn into long rallies. And you spend more time standing at the net than sprinting corner to corner.

    Here's the catch, though: doubles isn't just "easier singles." It's a different sport, and singles habits will get you killed out there. I've seen 3.5 singles players who can really hit the ball go out and lose 6-0, 6-0 in doubles — not because their strokes got worse, but because they never learned the language of doubles.

    Singles rewards the strongest individual. Doubles rewards the best team. A 3.5 player with a huge forehand will regularly lose to two 3.0 players who talk to each other, move as a unit, and trust each other completely. Your partner is your biggest weapon on that court — and also your biggest variable.

    Three things doubles has that singles doesn't, and why they matter more every year you play:

    A partner means you're not alone out there covering the alley — someone's got your back, literally. A net player means half your points get decided by an interception, not a grinding baseline rally. And because you've got a partner to aim at and an alley to avoid, placement and angles start to matter more than raw power.

    That last one is the good news for anyone over 50. Your power is going to decline a little every year — that's just physics. But your placement doesn't have to. It can hold steady, and honestly, it can keep improving well into your sixties and seventies. Doubles is built around exactly the skill that doesn't fade.

    So here's the order I'd suggest at 50+: doubles first, singles second. Not because singles is bad for you, but because doubles is safer. A singles match will have you covering 4-5 km, lunging, sliding, sometimes falling. A doubles match is more like 2-3 km, with a lot of that time spent standing at the net taking controlled volleys instead of chasing balls into the corners. And the recovery time is completely different — a 50+ player can play three doubles matches in a week and feel fine. Play one hard singles match at that age and you might need two days just to walk normally again.

    If you're thinking about giving up tennis at 50-something because singles has started to hurt — don't. Switch to doubles instead. The body that struggles through singles at 52 can absolutely thrive in doubles at 62. This is the format that keeps you on court for another twenty years.

    Communication & Movement: The Foundation¶

    Before we get into formations or patterns, I want to be clear about something: none of that matters if you and your partner aren't talking and moving together. This is the foundation everything else sits on. Most 3.5 teams don't lose because of bad shots — they lose because of silence. Two players calling out to each other constantly will beat two players hitting slightly better shots in total quiet, almost every time.

    The calls that actually matter¶

    You need five calls in your vocabulary, and you need to use them before the ball arrives, not after.

    "Mine!" or "Yours!" settles who's taking the ball — shout it early. "Switch!" fixes it when you both end up on the same side of the court. "Short!" flags that the opponent's ball has landed short and is attackable. "Lob!" warns your partner that the opponent is at net and you're about to go over their head. And a simple tap of the ball to your side before you serve tells your partner "my serve" without a word.

    Here's something worth planning for: hearing softens with age, and shouting "Mine!" loud enough to be heard across the court gets harder every year. The fix isn't to shout louder — it's to add hand signals as a backup. A fist behind your back means "mine." An open hand means "yours." Pointing at the alley means "cover it." Tapping your racquet on the ground tells your partner to let the ball go through and let it bounce.

    Moving like one player instead of two¶

    Three rules cover almost everything here. Move as a unit — if your partner shifts right, you shift left, at the same moment, like you're connected by a rope. When you're both chasing the same ball, take parallel paths rather than crossing directly into each other's way. And at the net, whoever's closer to the ball calls the shot — that's the net player leading, always.

    Think of it this way: you and your partner aren't two separate players who happen to share a court. You're one organism with two bodies. When your partner takes two steps right, you take two steps left, automatically, without thinking about it. The shape of your team stays constant even as the position moves around the court.

    The alley is IN — memorize the wider court¶

    This is the single biggest thing 3.5 players get wrong coming from singles: in doubles, both alleys are part of the court. Players who learned tennis in singles play as if the alley doesn't exist — they hit crosscourt returns straight into it and lose the point, or they leave it completely open because they're only covering the singles court in their heads.

    The rule that fixes this: when your partner gets pulled wide to cover a ball, you shift over to cover their side. The middle of the court is nobody's territory — it's the gap you have to own. Before your next match, actually walk the doubles lines. Get your body used to how much wider the real court is.

    Net or baseline — and an old idea about why it works¶

    After a good serve or return, you should be looking to get to the net — that's where you attack, intercept, and finish points. At the start of a point, or when things go sideways, the baseline is where you defend and reset. For most 3.5 players, the honest answer is that you should be at the net seventy percent of the time. Standing there and reacting is genuinely easier on the body than running the baseline all match.

    At 50+, the net is your friend for exactly this reason. Stand a meter or two off it, and you cover less ground, react faster, and end points with a single volley instead of grinding through a ten-shot rally. Your partner handles the lobs. The net is where this sport gets kindest to an older body.

    If you've spent any time around tai chi, there's a parallel here worth knowing. In tai chi, one side of a movement is thực — rooted, weighted, the axis — and the other is hư — light, free to move. In doubles, your baseline player is thực: rooted, holding the back of the court, setting up the next shot. Your net player is hư: light on their feet, free to poach, intercept, finish. And the roles aren't fixed — they rotate with the situation. When you're defending, the net player becomes more thực, anchoring position to shut down the lob. When you're attacking, that same player becomes more hư, poaching aggressively. The team keeps shifting between rooted and light depending on what the point needs.

    The Three Formations¶

    A formation is just where you and your partner stand at the start of the point. There are three worth knowing, and each one has a job.

    Australian (Both Up) — your default¶

    Server at the baseline, partner up at the net. This is the formation you should be in as the serving team roughly ninety percent of the time. It works because your net partner can step in and intercept the return, which forces the returner into a low, careful shot instead of a free swing — and from there, your net player finishes with a volley.

    Your net partner's job is specific: stand a meter or two off the net, leaning slightly toward the center service line rather than the alley. From there they can pick off anything down the middle or to their side, while you cover the alley. I think of this as the "hot dog" position — the center line is the bun, your partner's racquet is the hot dog, straddling the line, ready to swipe either direction. That's poach-ready.

    I-Formation — fun, but earn it first¶

    Here the setup hides information: server at the baseline as usual, but the net partner stands directly in front of the server rather than off to one side, so the returner has no idea which way they'll move. This forces a safer return down the middle or low, and your net player picks a side at the last possible second, usually off a signal — a touch on the left leg for "stay," the right leg for "cross" — that the returner can't see.

    I'll be honest with you: at 3.5, this is optional. Most points at this level are won with a solid Australian formation, a decent serve, and a well-timed poach — master that first. Bring in the I-formation once you and your partner have played twenty-plus matches together and have your signals down cold. Try it before that and you'll just end up running into each other.

    Both Back — a legitimate reset, not a failure¶

    Both players at the baseline, nobody at the net. This is your defensive option — use it when you're both struggling at net, when the opponents have a great poacher, or when the wind's too strong to trust a volley. Treat it as a temporary reset, though, not a season-long strategy — teams that live in Both Back for entire sets get picked apart by even a mediocre poacher.

    But there's a real use case at 50+: when your shoulder's sore, when your knees ache, when your body is telling you "no volleys today," Both Back is a completely legitimate choice. Playing it and winning sixty percent of your points beats gutting out Australian formation, tweaking your shoulder, and sitting out three months. That's not giving up — that's managing your body for the long game.

    Quick decision guide: Serving, and you and your partner have practiced I-formation over twenty-plus matches? Go with I-formation, or default back to Australian. Serving without that practice? Australian, every time. Body saying no to the net today? Both Back. Returning? You'll usually be in Australian, with your partner up front and you covering the lob duties from the back. Not serving or returning — just match whatever formation your opponents are running.

    The Four Patterns: How Points Actually Get Won¶

    A pattern is a repeatable sequence of shots that wins a typical doubles point, and there are four you should know cold.

    Serve + 1 — the workhorse¶

    This is the most common way points get won at 3.5, and it's not close — roughly half of all doubles points follow this shape. The server hits a wide slice to the backhand or aims at the T. The net partner reads the return: if it floats, they poach — crossing over and volleying down the middle. If it comes back low, they hold their ground and volley to the open court instead.

    The slice serve that pulls the returner off the court is the best serve in doubles for exactly this reason — it creates a "fat," floating return right into the middle, which is a poacher's dream. When the poach happens, the net partner crosses the center line at a sharp angle, ending up near the opposite sideline from where the serve went. The volley itself usually goes down the middle, into the gap between the two returners, or out to the alley if the returner's partner got pulled wide.

    Three things make the poach work. Read the return early — watch the returner's racquet, and if the swing looks loopy or the ball's ballooning up, that's your signal to go. Move on the rise — don't wait for the ball to arrive, start moving the instant the returner makes contact. And when you get there, volley to the middle — that's the biggest gap on the court, not the corners.

    Return + 1 — the mirror image¶

    Flip the roles and you get this pattern: the returner hits a low, deep return down the middle, and now it's their team attacking. Their net partner can poach off that return, while the baseline partner stays back to cover any lob.

    The safe doubles return is low, down the middle, with some slice or topspin on it — that denies the server's net player an easy interception. Here's a habit worth building deliberately: in singles you're crosscourt maybe ninety percent of the time on your return. In doubles, you want to go down the middle around sixty percent of the time instead, because it's simply harder to poach. If you're not sure what to do with a return at 50+, the chip or slice return down the middle is your default — it stays low, takes away the poach, and forces the server to hit up on the next ball.

    Lob-and-Recover — your escape hatch¶

    When both opponents are camped at the net and you're out of position with nothing good to hit, the lob is your best friend. It works because it forces both net players to turn around, scramble back, and hit an overhead — and most 3.5 overheads aren't reliable weapons. That buys you time to recover to the center and reset into Both Back or Australian.

    At 50+ specifically, go with the moonball — high, deep, and slow. It doesn't need pace at all; it just needs enough height that nobody can smash it. And here's a mental shift worth making: recreational players tend to think of the lob as purely defensive, as basically losing the point. That's wrong. A lob is a time-buying shot that resets the entire point, and pros use it constantly, including on offense.

    The lob also happens to be one of the friendliest shots in tennis for an aging body. It doesn't ask for speed, it doesn't ask for power — gravity does that work for you — a slightly mishit lob still usually works fine, and you can hit it from a high contact point without bending your knees much at all. Use it liberally.

    The Net Battle — when both teams are up¶

    This is the most exciting pattern in the sport: both teams at the net, the ball ping-ponging crosscourt, crosscourt, crosscourt, until someone finally has to make a decision — go for an angle, hit a body shot, or throw up a lob. Whoever cracks first loses the point.

    You've really got three options here. Crosscourt is your default — safe, deep, and it denies the sharp angle. A body shot at the opponent standing in the middle forces an awkward, split-second choice between forehand and backhand. And down-the-line is the killer shot — splits the two opponents and threads into the alley — but I'd leave it alone at 3.5. The alley's wide, the angle's sharp, and that shot needs a level of touch that mostly shows up at 4.5 and above. Go down-the-line at 3.5 in a net battle and you'll lose that point more often than you win it. Crosscourt is king at this level.

    This matters even more at 50+. Crosscourt volleys are easier to control because you've got more margin over the net, easier on the wrist because they need less snap, easier on the shoulder because they need less extension, and they're simply a higher-percentage shot given the shorter diagonal and lower net at that point. Hitting crosscourt at 50+ isn't playing it safe out of weakness — it's the smart read.

    Quick pattern guide: Your team serving, opponent returns low → Serve + 1, look for the poach. Your team returning, their net player's poaching → Return + 1, keep it down the middle. Both opponents at net, you're in trouble → Lob and recover. Both teams up, the ball's bouncing back and forth → Net Battle, crosscourt-crosscourt-crosscourt. Your body's telling you no to the net → Both Back, and that's fine.

    Drills, Cues & The 50+ Reality Check¶

    Five drills you can run this week¶

    The Communication Drill (with your partner, 5 minutes). Stand on court together. One of you calls it — every ten seconds, shout "Mine!" or "Yours!" or "Short!" or "Lob!" — and the other reacts. n't accuracy, it's rhythm: in a real match you'll be calling every three to five seconds, so get comfortable with that pace now. Once it feels natural, layer in the hand signals — fist behind the back for mine, open hand for yours.

    The "Move as a Unit" Drill (with your partner, 10 minutes). Stand together at the net, about two meters apart. One of you shuffles two steps right while the other shuffles two steps left, at exactly the same time. Do this twenty times, then add the return trip — shuffle out, recover back, shuffle out, recover — until your body stops treating this as two separate decisions and starts treating it as one.

    The Poach-and-Volley Drill (with your partner, 10 minutes). One of you serves, the other stands at net. After every single serve, the net player poaches across the center line and volleys it. Aim for ten poach-volleys in a row landing in. Once that's solid, have the server vary direction — wide, T, body — so the net player has to poach smartly off what they actually see, not just cross on autopilot.

    The Middle-Return Drill (with your partner, 10 minutes). Partner serves from the baseline, you return — and every single return has to go down the middle. Aim for ten in a row landing in the middle third of the service box. This is the drill that builds the "return down the middle" habit until it's automatic, because that's your doubles default going forward.

    The Lob-and-Recover Drill (with your partner, 5 minutes). Partner stands at net, you start at the baseline. Hit a high, deep lob over their head, then immediately recover to the center of the baseline. Aim for ten sequences where the lob clears the service line on their side and your recovery takes under two seconds. This is your 50+ escape hatch — it's worth training on purpose.

    The cues worth keeping in your head¶

    Talk to your partner every single point, every three to five seconds during play — silence is the enemy. Call "Mine!" or "Yours!" before the ball arrives, not after. Move as a unit, so when your partner goes right, you go left, automatically, after every shot. Serve to the T. Return down the middle. Poach when you read a floating ball. Lob when you're in trouble, high and deep, over both opponents. Stick to crosscourt in a net battle at this level. Cover the alley the moment your partner gets pulled wide. Default to standing at the net on most points. Remember that Both Back is a legitimate choice, not a failure. And if hearing's an issue, fist means mine, open hand means yours.

    Seven errors I see constantly at 3.5¶

    Silent partners who never talk and end up colliding — the fix is simply running the communication drill and committing to calling every few seconds. Both players stuck at the baseline with zero net presence — default to Australian and drill the "stand at the net" habit. Crosscourt returns that are really just a singles habit landing in the doubles alley — the middle-return drill and the 60% rule fix this fast. Down-the-line volleys attempted at 3.5 that sail into the alley — save that shot for 4.5 and up, crosscourt is your friend here. A net player who never poaches — the poach-and-volley drill, paired with reading the return early, breaks this habit. Poaching without a warning, which ends in a collision with your own partner — always call "I'm going!" before you cross. And lobs that land short at the service line, which just get smashed — the lob-and-recover drill, with an emphasis on depth, is the fix.

    What changes in your body at 50+, and the doubles-friendly answer¶

    Lunging for wide balls starts costing your knees and hips — trust your partner to cover the alley and lean harder on "move as a unit" so you're not the one lunging. Repeated overheads off lobs start wearing on the shoulder — favor the moonball lob yourself, and avoid smashing from well inside the baseline. Hearing softens, which makes "Mine!" harder to catch — hand signals solve this cleanly. Reaction time slows by a couple of milliseconds, which shows up as being late on poaches and returns — standing closer to the net buys back reaction time, and reading the return earlier helps too. Twisting to track lobs starts bothering the lower back — turn with your hips, not your spine. And recovery after a wide volley gets slower — crosscourt volleys keep you closer to your base position, which means less ground to make up.

    The single most important thing I can tell a 50+ player: doubles adds ten to twenty years to your tennis life. Players who stick to singles-only past 50 often quit by 60. Players who make the switch to doubles are still out there playing at 70. This isn't a lesser version of the sport — it's the long game.

    The tai chi thread, if that's useful to you¶

    A few principles from tai chi map onto this surprisingly well, if you want a mental framework to hang it on. Hư-Thực (light-rooted) is your net player being light and mobile while your baseline partner stays rooted. Tương Hỗ (mutual support) is the idea that your team is one organism in two bodies, supporting each other constantly. Hợp Nhất (unity) is "move as a unit" in a single word — one shape, not two players. Thính Kình (listening power) is reading the opponent's return and feeling the moment to poach rather than thinking your way there. Phóng Tùng (relaxation) is the reminder that communication only actually works when both of you are relaxed enough to speak up. And Lã Kình (yielding) is the lob itself — yielding to pressure instead of fighting it, redirecting the point back over the net players' heads.

    Your mental checklist before every point¶

    Before the point starts: talk to your partner — "your serve," "my serve," "ready?" Settle your position — Australian by default, I-formation if you've earned it, Both Back if your body needs it. And remind yourself to get to the net whenever you can.

    As the point begins: if you're serving, aim for the T or a wide slice. If you're returning, go down the middle around sixty percent of the time. If you're at net, watch the returner's racquet and get ready to poach.

    During the point: call "Mine!" or "Yours!" before the ball gets there. Keep moving as a unit — partner right, you left. Stick to crosscourt volleys rather than down-the-line at this level.

    If things go sideways: lob it high and deep, over both opponents if they're up. Recover to center immediately after. Reset into Australian or Both Back.

    After the point: say something to your partner — "good shot," "mine was too high," "let's poach more." Then reset and go again.
