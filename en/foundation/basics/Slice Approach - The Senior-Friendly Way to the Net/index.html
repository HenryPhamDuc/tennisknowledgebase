    The Slice Approach — Your Senior-Friendly Way to the Net¶

    Watch a 3.5 player get a short ball. Their eyes light up. They swing. The ball lands in the net, or sails long, or — if they're lucky — lands short, and they just stand there at the baseline looking confused about what to do next.

    Here's the truth: the approach shot is the most under-practiced pattern in recreational tennis. Most players have no idea how to actually approach. They hit "a good shot" and then wonder why they can't capitalize on it.

    The real issue is that the approach isn't one shot — it's a four-step pattern. Step one: recognize the right ball. Step two: hit the slice approach. Step three: transition to the net. Step four: finish with a volley. Skip any one of these steps, and the whole pattern falls apart.

    The slice approach is the only approach shot I'll recommend at your stage, because it's the slowest, lowest-stress, and most accurate option available to you.

    Think of it like chess. Moving a pawn forward is "the approach" — but you don't just push the pawn blindly. You check first: is the position right? Is the opponent's king exposed? Tennis works the same way. The slice is your pawn push, but the position has to be right before you make the move. A well-timed approach with a mediocre slice beats a poorly-timed approach with a perfect one, every time. The decision comes before the shot.

    The Decision: Four Questions to Ask First¶

    Before you approach, run through these four questions:

    1. Is the ball short — landing inside the service box? If not, stay at the baseline. Don't approach on a deep ball.
    2. Is your opponent off-balance or still recovering? If they're already set, an approach just invites a passing shot. Wait for a better ball.
    3. Can you hit a deep slice — landing within about a meter of the baseline? If not, don't approach — a short approach is a free sitting ball for your opponent at the net.
    4. Is the open court visible? If you can't see where the volley should go, you'll send it to the wrong place.

    Most players at your level skip questions one and two entirely — they see a "hittable" ball and just charge the net. But if the ball is deep, or your opponent's already set, you're running straight into a passing shot. The approach only works when all four answers are yes. If even one is no, hit another groundstroke and wait for a better opportunity.

    Why Slice, Not Topspin¶

    The slice approach is the only one I recommend past 50, and here's why:

    • Low net clearance. It stays low over the net, and a low approach is harder to pass than a high one — your opponent has to hit up to get past you, which is a much tougher shot than hitting across.
    • Underspin keeps it low after the bounce. A sliced approach skids rather than sitting up, which shrinks your opponent's margin for error on the pass.
    • Slow racket speed. It's a small, controlled swing — less shoulder load, less back strain, less knee stress. At your stage, slice is simply the joint-friendly way to approach.
    • High accuracy. The natural arc of a slice makes depth control easy. A topspin approach demands precise margin — clear the net and land deep, both at once. Slice gives you a much wider window to work with.

    A topspin approach is a Federer specialty, but at your level the risk-reward doesn't add up: a topspin approach that lands short is just a free passing shot handed to your opponent.

    Two Slice Approaches: Backhand Default, Forehand as a Next Step¶

    Every player eventually needs both sides covered, but the two aren't equal at your stage.

    Lead with the backhand slice approach, always. Practice it for two or three months until it's automatic, then add the forehand version as a next step once the situation calls for it. Don't try to learn both at once — the footwork is the same, but the grip and swing path are different, and mixing them up while learning slows down both.

    The Backhand Slice Approach — Your Default¶

    Grip and stance. Continental — same as your volley, your slice backhand, your lob. This is the single most important decision here: a Semi-Western grip on the approach will leave the face open at contact and send the ball long. Stance is open or semi-open, same as your slice backhand — it lets your chest rotate up and through, which is what gives you the underspin that keeps the ball low.

    The ball needs to bounce at least once before you hit it — no exceptions. Trying to volley an approach straight out of the air is for pros with elite reactions. At your level, you need that bounce to slow the ball down. It's a simple rule, but it kills about 80% of bad approaches on its own: a bounced ball gives you half a second of reaction time — enough to set your feet, turn your shoulder, and hit it clean.

    Contact. Waist-to-chest height, in front of your body — same height as a regular slice backhand, but with more underspin, so the ball stays lower after the bounce. Racket face open, hood angle — Continental grip plus a slight wrist layback gets you there, face open 30-45 degrees from vertical. The swing path goes high to low, brushing down the back of the ball — racket starts at shoulder height and ends at waist height, drawing a line downward as the strings brush under the ball.

    Picture the racket face as a frying pan and the ball as a pancake you're flipping. The pan starts high at your shoulder, catches the ball, and tilts down to flip it low over the net with underspin. The pancake doesn't go very high — it goes far, low, and lands deep. That's the whole shot.

    Aim. Cross-court, into the deep corner. For a right-hander approaching from the ad side, that's the deuce corner — the geometrically deepest target available. Why cross-court rather than down the line? Because it forces the longer passing shot on your opponent. A down-the-line approach gives them the shorter, easier pass. Always make them hit the longer ball.

    Depth matters as much as direction — aim to land within about a meter of the baseline. A short approach is just a gift-wrapped passing shot. And don't try to hit a "moon" approach — a high, slow, lob-like ball. That just gives your opponent time to set up. Low and deep, never high and deep.

    Think of the approach as a trap. You're setting your opponent up to pass into a low, fast volley. If the approach sits up high, the trap fails — they can hit a comfortable passing shot, or even lob over your head. If it stays low, they're forced to hit up to get it past you, and a ball hit upward from well behind the baseline against a player already at net is a genuinely difficult shot.

    The Forehand Slice Approach — Your Next Step¶

    This one's a different stroke entirely. Your regular Semi-Western forehand grip doesn't slice naturally, so you have to change grips before you can hit it.

    Grip and stance. Switch to Continental the moment the approach opportunity shows up on your forehand — grip change first, then approach. That change costs you roughly 0.3-0.5 seconds, which is exactly why the backhand version stays your default (no grip change needed there at all). Stance is open, same principle as the backhand version, letting your chest rotate through.

    Contact. Slightly higher than the backhand version — chest height rather than waist. Your forehand shoulder has more natural freedom to swing, so a higher contact point keeps things under control. Everything else matches the backhand approach: high-to-low path, open hood-angle face, strings brushing the back of the ball.

    Aim. Down the line — the one exception to the cross-court rule. Your forehand's natural angle already runs cross-court, so slicing cross-court off the forehand tends to land short. Down the line keeps this a controlled, deep ball instead.

    The trap most players fall into here is trying to approach on the forehand with their regular Semi-Western grip and a topspin swing. That produces a high, loopy ball that lands short of the service line — an easy target for a passing shot. The fix is simply switching to Continental and slicing. Yes, the grip change costs a fraction of a second. But the difference between a high, loopy approach that gets passed and a low slice approach that sets up an easy volley is the difference between losing the point and winning it.

    The Transition: How You Actually Get to the Net¶

    This is where most approaches actually die. Players hit a beautiful slice approach and then just... stop at the baseline. Or shuffle toward the net in slow motion and arrive after the opponent's already passed them.

    The transition is its own skill, separate from the shot itself. The swing is over — the footwork is a new task, with its own four steps:

    1. Push off your back foot as you make contact with the slice.
    2. Side-shuffle for 2-3 steps to recover your balance.
    3. Sprint diagonally toward the net.
    4. Split-step at the net the instant your opponent makes contact with the passing shot.

    Think of the whole transition as a rebound — like a basketball bouncing off the backboard. The slice approach is the bounce, the recovery shuffle is the settle, and the diagonal sprint is the new direction. Don't try to sprint straight from contact — your body's still rotating and your feet are still planted from the shot. Sprint immediately and you'll trip over your own feet. Shuffle first, then sprint.

    The Recovery Shuffle — Your Most-Skipped Step¶

    Most players at your level think "I hit the approach, now sprint" — that's the wrong instinct. The instant after contact, your body's still rotated and your weight's still forward. Sprint immediately and you'll be off-balance at the net, and an off-balance player at net hits a weak volley almost every time.

    Side-shuffle 2-3 steps, in the direction opposite to where you just hit the ball. That settles your weight, brings your feet back underneath you, and sets you up for the sprint. It costs about half a second — and your opponent needs a full 1-1.5 seconds just to set up and swing at the passing shot. You genuinely have time for this.

    Put the full sequence together: side-shuffle (0.3-0.5 sec) → diagonal sprint (0.5-0.8 sec) → split-step at the net (0.1 sec). Total: 1-1.5 seconds — which lines up almost exactly with how long your opponent needs to hit their pass. You arrive at the net right as the ball arrives. That's the timing you're aiming for.

    The Diagonal Sprint¶

    This part's genuinely fun — the explosive moment where you push off, accelerate, and arrive at net. The direction is diagonal, toward your volley position: a cross-court backhand approach sends your sprint forward and to your right (for a right-hander approaching from the ad side); a down-the-line forehand approach sends it forward and to your left.

    The same 70/30 weight rule from volleying applies here — at the net, 70% of your weight should sit on your front, net-side leg. That's the predator stance: forward, low, ready. Arrive standing tall, and you're not actually in volley position — you're still in transition, and you'll be late on the first ball.

    Past 50, this sprint is honestly the riskiest part of the whole approach. The acceleration loads your knees, your back, and your heart all at once. Don't sprint at 100% — sprint at about 70%. A controlled approach that arrives 0.3 seconds later beats a sprint that pulls a hamstring or spikes your heart rate to 180. Smooth beats fast, every time, at your stage.

    The Volley Finish¶

    You've hit the slice, transitioned to the net, split-stepped — the pass is in the air. Now what?

    Good news: this is the exact same volley you already know. Continental grip, L-shape wrist, no backswing, a short 15-centimeter punch, frozen wrist through contact. Nothing new mechanically. The approach only adds one thing — where you aim.

    The rule is simple geometry: if you approached cross-court, your opponent's pass most likely comes down the line — the shorter angle available to them — which leaves cross-court open for your volley. If you approached down the line, expect their pass cross-court, leaving down the line open for you. Always volley into the open court — the side opposite of where you approached, since your opponent's pass takes away the angle you came from.

    Think of it as a seesaw. You approach to one side, your opponent passes to the other. The seesaw always tips away from where the ball came from — and your volley goes wherever it tips.

    Reading the incoming pass. Hard, right at your body — block it, no swing, cross-court is safest. At or above net height — punch volley, cross-court by default. Below net height but still reachable — slice volley, deep into the corner. At your feet — half-volley, knees bent deep, a scooping motion. A lob over your head — track it back for a controlled overhead, or a lob volley if you can't get there in time.

    At your level, stick to two default patterns: punch cross-court when the open court is available and you can drive it firmly enough that your opponent has no realistic chance to retrieve it, or block and wait deep when the pass comes in hot and you just need to reset rather than force anything. A drop volley is a nice surprise weapon once you're comfortable, but it's not your bread-and-butter shot yet — save it for when your opponent is clearly stuck well behind the baseline.

    Five Drills for This Week¶

    1. Slice Approach Triangulation — solo, 5 minutes a day. Set up three points (real or imagined): one at the service line, one at the net, one at the far baseline corner. Hit a backhand slice deep cross-court to the first point. Side-shuffle two steps, then sprint diagonally to the second. Air-swing a volley toward the third. Goal: 10 consecutive full sequences. This builds the geometry of the whole approach into your body.

    2. Live Ball Approach — with a partner, 15 minutes. Rally normally until your partner feeds a short ball. Run the four questions — if all yes, hit a backhand slice approach cross-court and move in. Your partner hits a passing shot down the line (the shorter angle). You finish with a cross-court volley, or block deep if the pass comes in hot. Goal: 5 successful approaches ending in either a volley winner or a forced error.

    3. Side-Shuffle + Sprint Timing — solo, 3 minutes a day. Stand at the center mark. On your own count of "one," side-shuffle two steps. On "two," sprint forward five steps. On "three," split-step. Repeat 10 times. This drills the rhythm directly: shuffle, sprint, split — 1 to 1.5 seconds total.

    4. Forehand Grip-Change Drill — solo, 2 minutes a day. Start in your normal Semi-Western forehand grip. On "one," switch to Continental. On "two," air-swing a forehand slice approach. On "three," switch back. Goal: 10 grip changes in 30 seconds, until it stops requiring thought.

    5. Approach Decision Shadow Drill — solo, 3 minutes a day. Have a friend call out one of the four decision questions at random. Step forward if the answer's yes, step back if it's no. Repeat for 30 seconds. Most missed approaches at your level fail because the decision was wrong, not the shot — this drill trains the decision first.

    The Five Cues to Remember¶

    1. "Four questions first." Decide, then hit.
    2. "Frying pan." Face open, ball is a pancake you're flipping low over the net.
    3. "Shake off the contact." Side-shuffle 2-3 steps before you sprint.
    4. "70% sprint, not 100%." Smooth beats fast.
    5. "Open court is the volley." Wherever the seesaw tips, that's your target.

    Seven Mistakes I See Constantly¶

    Mistake What it looks like The fix
    Approaching on a deep ball Trying to approach from the baseline Wait for the short ball — the 1-bounce rule
    Topspin approach Regular Semi-Western forehand used as the approach Switch to Continental and slice
    Short approach Slice lands in the service box instead of deep Aim within a meter of the baseline
    Sprinting straight from contact Running forward the instant you hit Side-shuffle 2-3 steps first, then sprint
    Sprinting at 100% Full sprint, arriving exhausted at net Sprint at 70% — smooth, not fast
    Wrong volley aim Volleying back to the side you approached from Open court is the opposite side
    Accidental drop volley Attempting one while out of position Default to punch-cross or block-wait instead

    The 50+ Reality Check¶

    Knee strain on the sprint. A full-speed sprint loads both knees and lower back. Cap it at 70% — smooth, not fast.

    Heart rate spikes. The approach sprint can push your heart rate to 170-180. Approach once or twice a game, not on every point — pick your moments.

    Shoulder strain on the slice. Comes from too long a swing. The "frying pan" cue keeps it small and controlled.

    Back strain on the shuffle. Usually from bending at the waist instead of the hips during the recovery shuffle. Bend at the hips, let your knees do the work.

    Losing confidence after a missed approach. One bad approach doesn't mean the decision was wrong — if all four questions were genuinely yes, the read was right and the execution just needs another rep. Reset and move on.

    Tennis elbow on the slice. Usually from brushing too hard trying to add pace. Keep the grip light — 3-4 out of 10 — and the swing small. The slice was never about power.

    Backhand overhead exposure. Watch for your opponent's cross-court pass sailing over your backhand shoulder. Cover the down-the-line pass first, since it's the shorter, more likely shot — cross-court is the longer ball.

    The single most important thing to take from all of this: the slice approach is the most joint-friendly approach shot in tennis. No shoulder load, because the swing's small. No back strain, thanks to the open stance. No knee stress, if you keep that sprint at 70%. At your stage, this isn't really a tactical choice — it's a longevity choice. A player who approaches with slice once or twice a set is protecting their body for the next twenty years of tennis.
