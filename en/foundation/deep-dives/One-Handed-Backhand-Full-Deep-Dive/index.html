    The One-Handed Backhand: The Silk, The Cannon & The Quiet Eye¶

    An older recreational player walked onto court once and asked me, "Should I learn the one-handed backhand?" The honest answer in 2026: probably not for power shots. Yes for slice. And maybe — maybe — for topspin, if your shoulder says yes and your ego says no.

    This is really for three kinds of players: people who already have a one-hander and want to refine it without wrecking their shoulder; people who want the one-handed SLICE specifically, the elegant, shoulder-friendly weapon that most players past 50 should be using more; and coaches or students who want the elite biomechanics translated down to something a 3.5 shoulder can actually use.

    Here's the 50+ decision, laid out plainly. If your dominant shoulder has no injury history and you've been playing 10+ years, you have real range here — go ahead and borrow from all three pro models below. If it's a little stiff but not impinged, lean toward slice as your daily shot and treat topspin as an occasional guest. If you've got a history of impingement, rotator cuff strain, or chronic tightness, the topspin one-hander is off the table — two hands for power, one hand for slice only.

    The pro paper this is based on analyzed three elite one-handed backhands — Dimitrov, Wawrinka, Federer — each a valid solution to the same problem, with very different trade-offs. At 3.5, you don't pick a favorite pro to imitate wholesale. You pick the individual s that are actually safe for your shoulder. Dimitrov's fluid C-loop trades on hip rotation and the elastic recoil of that big "C" shape — medium shoulder load, and the is "let the racket head fall like silk." Wawrinka's explosive linear game runs on torso unwind and a deep left-elbow pull — high shoulder load, and the that's actually safe to copy is the linear weight transfer through the front foot, not the elbow pull itself. Federer's efficient compact style runs on precision, a quiet eye, and a nearly straight arm — low-to-medium load, and the full package (quiet eye, heel-to-toe, close-to-straight arm) is worth copying.

    The one-handed backhand is, honestly, the most internally-coordinated stroke in tennis. It asks the same thing tai chi push-hands asks: one side leads, one side roots, the spine works as a door hinge, and the power comes from the Dantian — your body's movement hub in the lower abdomen — loading into the Mingmen, the "back power gate" in your lower spine, and releasing through the chest. On a two-hander, the non-dominant hand adds a second engine of mechanical stability. On a one-hander, that second engine is gone — you have to build the engine inside. That's exactly why a good one-hander looks beautiful: it's all core.

    One warning before we get into technique. The pro paper documents frame counts and joint angles that took each of these players 15+ years to develop. The single biggest mistake a 3.5 player can make is trying to copy the extreme version of any of these takebacks — Dimitrov's deep chin-on-shoulder turn, Wawrinka's deep left-elbow pull, Federer's fully straight arm. Each of those took over 10,000 hours to build safely. Past 50, you want the minimum effective dose of each — and you skip the rest.

    The Grip and Unit Turn¶

    Grip is Continental, full stop, no exceptions. The V between your thumb and index sits on bevel #2 — the same bevel you'd use for the volley, the slice, or the overhead. Why Continental and not Semi-Western? On a one-hander, you don't have a second hand bracing the face. Eastern or Semi-Western would leave the face open at contact and the ball would sail long. Continental keeps the face slightly closed — exactly right for a single-arm contact. Try this check: hold the racket as if shaking hands with it, then rotate your hand slightly counter-clockwise until the V sits on the top-left bevel. That's Continental. Another way to verify it: imagine you're hammering a nail into the court in front of you. The face of that "hammer" should point toward the side fence, not up at the sky. If it's pointing skyward, your grip has drifted toward Eastern — fix it.

    The unit turn looks different here than on the two-hander, and this is the single biggest visual difference between the setups. On a two-hander, both hands come to chest height and form a diamond. On a one-hander, your top hand stays empty — it never touches the racket during the load. Instead it has exactly one job: point at the ball as you turn. Think of it as a "D-minus-top" position — the same D-shape as the two-hander's setup, just with the top hand removed and repurposed as a sighting device. That left hand helps the unit turn (the elbow tucks toward the right hip as the shoulders rotate, and the racket gets "tossed" back by the shoulder turn, not by the arm pulling it), it stays up and points at the incoming ball right through the turn and into the early swing, and then it releases early in the forward swing — it does not stay on the racket.

    Try this shape in a mirror: stand sideways, right shoulder to the net, racket up and back with your right arm bent about 90° at the elbow — an L-shape from shoulder to racket head. Your left arm is also bent at 90°, hand at chin height, pointing at the ball you imagine coming in, left elbow close to the body. The whole shape should look like a goalie readying for a penalty kick.

    That unit turn needs to arrive on time, and the sequencing matters more here than on a two-hander. Split-step first, then turn — that's the same L2 cue from the forehand, but it's even more critical on the one-hander because you have less margin for error at contact. A late turn on a two-hander often still works because the second hand stabilizes things. A late turn on a one-hander usually produces a shank or a slice that dives into the net. The sequence: see the ball leave your opponent's strings, hop (a small, knees-loaded split-step), turn your shoulders to the right — chin on right shoulder, right arm in that L-shape, left hand pointing — and then settle your eyes on the contact zone, which sets up the quiet eye you'll use later.

    One drill worth building daily. The Wall Mirror Setup: stand a meter from a wall or mirror and do the unit turn ten times — hop, turn, L-shape, left hand pointing — holding each position for two seconds. Check yourself: chin on right shoulder? Racket head up? Left hand pointing at the wall? Left elbow tucked? Ten reps a day and your body starts treating the one-hander's setup as seriously as it already treats the forehand's.

    The Four-Layer System on One Arm¶

    The same four layers you know from the forehand — swing, setup, axis, feel — apply here too, but the load distribution changes completely. On a two-hander the load is shared across two arms. On a one-hander, all of it sits on your right shoulder and the right side of your core. That's exactly why the axis and feel layers matter even more here, not less.

    The swing. The L shape lives on the one-hander too — the angle between your right forearm and the racket shaft stays bent almost to contact. At 3.5, straightening that arm too early is the single biggest swing fix available; it's the one-handed equivalent of forehand arm-swinging, and it produces a weak, late, injury-prone shot. Keep the 70/30 rhythm, even more deliberately here — 70% slow (the racket head dropping, the left elbow tucking, the chest loading) and 30% fast (chest rotation and arm release, nothing more).

    You've genuinely got two valid takeback shapes to choose from at this level. The C-loop, Dimitrov's shape, drops the racket head in a large C below the ball with a deep unit turn and a slightly closed face — the cue is "let the racket head fall like silk, gravity does the work," and it looks beautiful on players with a deep, committed turn, though the deep chin-on-shoulder position does load the neck past 50. The slot, Federer's shape, keeps the racket head more compact, dropping to about waist-to-chest height slightly behind the body — the cue here is "racket head below the ball, not behind the head," and this is genuinely your 50+ default: smaller takeback, less shoulder load, easier timing. The slot needs less shoulder external rotation, less neck turn, and less upper-back arch than the C-loop — it's Federer's efficiency principle in practice, achieving the same contact with less travel and less risk. Past 50, that's the difference between a one-hander you can play twice a week and one that puts you on the shelf.

    Either way, the drop itself is the real engine. From the L-shape load, the racket head falls on its own edge, below the wrist, into whichever position you're using. Your hand does not pull it down — gravity does the work. This matters specifically because of how it loads your shoulder: a pulled-down racket creates active shoulder depression, where the muscles actively drag the arm down. A dropped racket creates passive loading instead — the arm just hangs in the socket while the chest does the lifting. Passive loading is gentle. Active loading is what causes impingement over ten thousand reps. Dimitrov calls this the "silk" cue: imagine the racket head is a piece of silk falling through the air — you don't pull silk, you just let it fall. Watch his C-loop in slow motion and you'll see it: the racket head describes a large, smooth arc, reaches its lowest point, pauses for a fraction of a second — that pause is the loading, like a whip being drawn back before the crack — and then accelerates forward.

    The axis. This is the deepest layer of the one-hander. Think of your spine as a door hinge, not a barrel roll. Rotation here is bigger than on a two-hander because there's no second arm to limit it, and the real risk is over-rotating the upper spine and neck, which loads both. The door-hinge principle: hips rotate first, low to high, the chest follows, the shoulders follow the chest, the arm follows the shoulders — each segment starting only after the one below it, the kinetic chain running from the ground up. For a right-hander, your left side is now the rooted axis and your right side is free to fly — the right foot works almost like a kickstand, stepping in while the left leg rotates and the chest opens.

    In tai chi this is called Mở Mingmen — opening the back power gate. It's the sensation of expansion through the lower back as the hips load into a turn. On the takeback, your right hip rotates back, your left hip stays forward, and your lower back opens — expands — as it stores elastic energy. On the forward swing, that same lower back closes, releasing what it stored, as the hips unwind. This is genuinely the one-hander's secret: most 3.5 players keep the lower back stiff and let the rotation happen in the upper spine and neck instead. Opening the Mingmen is what actually allows the hip-shoulder separation that creates racket head speed.

    The feel. The quiet eye is Federer's contribution and it costs you nothing to copy — worth a genuine 20% of your ball-striking consistency. After contact, your eyes stay on the contact point for about 0.2 seconds, head barely moving. That stillness gives your cerebellum 200 extra milliseconds of feedback to refine your next shot — the cheapest, fastest upgrade available in the sport. In practice only, not in matches, you can extend this into a two-second pause: after every shot you hit well, freeze the follow-through and count "one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi." That locks the good position into your body's memory rather than just into a mental replay. In an actual match, hold for the quiet 0.2 seconds and move on.

    Try this drill. Have a friend feed you ten balls. Your only job: hit, hold the follow-through, count "one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi," then turn to track the next ball. After ten reps, switch — hit, then immediately turn your head to track. Notice the difference: the first version feels slow but produces far more consistent contact.

    The Three Pros: What to Copy, What to Leave¶

    This is the chapter where most 3.5 players go wrong — they pick a favorite pro off the television and try to copy the extreme version of that player's takeback. Here's the 3.5-level translation of what's actually worth taking from each.

    Dimitrov, the fluid artist. His signature is a racket head brought high, sometimes above the head, then dropped in a large C below the ball, with a deep unit turn — chin on right shoulder, left shoulder nearly perpendicular to the net — and contact out in front, arm nearly extended, wrist slightly laid back. Power comes from hip rotation, shoulder whip, and the elastic recoil of the C itself, and the whole thing looks like silk because every joint moves in sequence with no jerks or pauses. He also drops his center of gravity significantly on forced shots, right knee nearly touching the court, which loads the legs as springs so the upward drive comes from the legs rather than the arm.

    Worth copying: the C-loop drop cue itself — let the racket head fall like silk, then accelerate through the ball. It's the easiest pro cue to copy, and even at 3.5, dropping the head in a C shape rather than pulling it back reduces shoulder load and adds racket head speed. Also worth copying: the drop-pause-accelerate rhythm. At the bottom of the C, the racket head reaches its lowest point and pauses for a fraction of a second — that's the elastic loading, and skipping it means skipping the spring.

    Worth skipping: the deep chin-on-shoulder unit turn, which loads the cervical spine and upper trapezius past 50 — Dimitrov can do it because of fifteen-plus years of neck conditioning, so instead turn your shoulders right while keeping your chin closer to neutral, letting the chest turn without the head craning. Also skip the very deep knee bend on every shot — save that for balls that have genuinely pulled you wide, and use a normal athletic stance on neutral balls.

    Wawrinka, the explosive powerhouse. His signature is aggressive early preparation, a deep pull-back of the left elbow behind the back, and a linear weight transfer with the front foot angled at 45° to the net; contact is high with the racket angled to add heavy topspin. Power comes from the torso unwinding — the deep left elbow and the torso coil store energy that releases as the hips and shoulders unwind in sequence, ground through legs through hips through torso through shoulder through arm through racket through ball. It's the sound his backhand makes on contact — a heavy signature thwack — that broadcasters love, and that sound is the sound of maximum kinetic-chain transfer.

    Here's the honest warning: the deep left elbow pull is the single most shoulder-stressful cue documented in the pro paper this is based on. Wawrinka is 6'3", 187 lbs, and has been hitting this way since he was twelve — his shoulders, rotator cuffs, and thoracic spine are conditioned specifically for this load. Past 50, that same deep pull produces real shoulder impingement risk (extreme external rotation plus abduction narrows the space under the shoulder blade), rotator cuff strain (the supraspinatus and infraspinatus load in a stretched position), upper-back tightness (the thoracic spine has to rotate more than a normal one-hander needs), and lower-back pain (the deep coil loads the L4-L5 discs — and even Wawrinka himself has had multiple back surgeries).

    What's actually safe to borrow: the linear weight transfer itself — front foot stepping in at 45° to the net, weight moving forward, back foot pushing off. It's "tank tread" footwork, the body moving forward into the shot rather than just rotating, and it's genuinely copyable past 50. Also copyable: the early preparation — Wawrinka starts his takeback the instant he reads the ball's direction, and "turn early, hit late" is the easiest Wawrinka there is, useful precisely because the one-hander has less margin for error than the two-hander. And the 45° front foot angle helps too, though you don't need the full 45° — even 20-30° opens the hips enough to let the chest rotate freely; a closed stance with the foot parallel to the baseline just blocks that rotation. What to skip entirely: the deep left elbow pull. Keep your left elbow close to your body in the load position — a slight pull-back is fine, it stores some elastic energy — but the extreme version genuinely belongs to 25-year-old shoulders.

    Federer, the effortless efficiency machine. His signature is a compact "slot" takeback (racket head below the ball's trajectory, face slightly closed), a straight arm at contact, a heel-to-toe step into the court, and exceptional head stability — the quiet eye, 0.2 seconds after contact. Power comes from precision rather than size: his racket head speed at contact is actually lower than Dimitrov's or Wawrinka's, but his contact point is more consistent, his placement more precise, and his efficiency is exactly what lets him sustain the level across five-hour matches. The straight arm at contact is a genuine long lever that maximizes mechanical advantage, but it took 25+ years of training to develop safely — past 50, a slightly bent arm, 15-20° of flex, is the safer trade.

    Nearly the whole Federer package is worth copying, and it's your rational 50+ default. The compact slot takeback — racket head dropping to about waist-to-chest height, slightly behind the body — is the minimum effective takeback: less travel, less shoulder load, easier timing. The heel-to-toe step, right foot striking heel-first then rolling to the toe as you load, is the safest footwork available past 50 — small, controlled, gradual knee loading, no lunging, no sudden stops; save the deep lunges for genuinely wide balls. The quiet eye, that 0.2 seconds of head stillness after contact, is the cheapest and fastest upgrade in tennis — even 3.5 players see a 15-20% jump in ball-striking consistency after four weeks of practicing it. And the whole efficiency mindset is worth internalizing on its own: Federer wins by not doing more than necessary — smaller takeback, less racket head speed, more placement. At 3.5, copy the efficiency, not the extremes. The one thing to modify: the fully straight arm. Use 15-20° of elbow flex instead — you lose about 5% of mechanical advantage and gain roughly 50% of shoulder safety, which is a trade worth making every time.

    Try this drill daily, five minutes. Stand at the baseline with no ball. Take the racket back to the slot position — head below where the ball would be, face slightly closed, right arm bent about 90° at the elbow, left hand pointing at the imaginary ball. Hold that slot for two seconds and check: face slightly closed? Head below the ball? Left hand pointing? Then swing forward slowly, bending your right elbow slightly — 15-20°, not straight, not fully bent, just almost straight — as you reach the contact zone. Follow through over your right shoulder, freeze, and count "one-Mississippi, two-Mississippi."

    The quick reference for what not to copy: skip Dimitrov's deep chin-on-shoulder turn (loads the cervical spine — 50+ necks can't take it) and his very deep knee bend on every shot (wastes energy; save it for forced wide balls). Skip Wawrinka's deep left elbow pull entirely (the number one shoulder injury risk in the whole paper), his extreme 45° front foot angle (20-30° is enough and causes less hip strain), and his very high contact point with a 45° racket (that requires a 6'3" reach most of us don't have). Skip Federer's fully straight arm at contact (25+ years of shoulder conditioning behind it — a slight flex is safer). And across all three, skip the very wide racket path in general — 3.5 players don't have the timing for it, and the slot position is genuinely enough.

    The One-Handed Slice: Your Shoulder Insurance¶

    The slice is the one one-handed shot that essentially every player past 50 should learn, regardless of shoulder history. It's the one-hander's insurance policy — same grip as your volley, same high-to-low windshield-wiper motion, same underspin that disrupts rhythm, buys time, and works for defense, low balls, approach shots, and returning big serves.

    Here's what actually separates the slice from the topspin one-hander, layer by layer: the grip is Continental either way, but the slice motion is different even with the same grip. The takeback is high, racket head above the shoulder, rather than the slot or C-loop's racket-head-up position. The swing path runs high-to-low rather than low-to-high. Contact lands at waist height on the way down rather than waist-to-chest in front. The face sits open, 30-45° like a hood, rather than slightly closed and neutral. The finish is low, across the body, near the opposite hip, rather than up over the right shoulder. The wrist stays fully locked with no pronation at all. And critically, the shoulder load is genuinely low — the swing is short and downward rather than rotational, which is exactly why this shot is shoulder-friendly in the first place.

    Here's the key insight worth sitting with: on the one-handed slice, the power comes from gravity, not your arm. The high takeback creates potential energy; the swing simply releases it. Your body weight transfers forward; the arm is just the delivery mechanism. That's precisely why the shoulder doesn't have to do much work here — the legs do. Compare that to the one-handed topspin, where the shoulder does most of the rotational work, rotating 90°+ in about 0.2 seconds. That's exactly where the impingement risk on the topspin version comes from.

    Think of the whole motion as "open the door, walk through." The takeback is the door swinging open — racket head goes up high, above the lead shoulder, right arm opens, body starts to turn. The swing is the door swinging closed — racket head comes down and across the body, face staying open, contact happening in the middle of that swinging motion. You walk through the door at contact: your weight transfers forward, your left foot plants, your right shoulder rotates through, and the ball comes off with underspin and sidespin together — floating slightly forward before it drops. A windshield wiper is the cleanest image for this: two ends, one high takeback and one low finish, the wiper passing through the middle where contact happens, the face angled the whole way through.

    A handful of cues worth carrying: Continental grip, high takeback. Open like a hood. Soft hands, firm contact. Slice means under the ball. Door opens, door closes. Walk through at contact. Listen for the fwoosh.

    The same four situations apply here as elsewhere: under pressure, just get it back deep and buy time to recover. On an approach, slice low and move forward, since the low ball forces your opponent into a defensive slice or lob. Returning a big serve, slice is your friend — the underspin absorbs their pace and lands short, taking time away from the server. And after a topspin rally, a slice disrupts the rhythm entirely, changing the spin and throwing off their timing.

    A drill worth three times a week, ten minutes. Stand 4 meters from a wall and rally one-handed slice only, slow pace, aiming for 20 consecutive balls that bounce below waist height on your side. Listen for the sound — a clean slice sounds like "fwoosh," a flat hit sounds like "thwack." Hit this one with the right hand only, no left hand on the racket at all — that's different from the two-handed slice, where both hands stay on. At 3.5, it's worth trying both versions and seeing which gives you more consistency; most players past 50 end up preferring the one-handed slice because having the left hand free actually helps with balance.

    Drills, Cues, and Care for the Shoulder Past 50¶

    Seven drills worth building. The Wall Mirror Setup (daily, 3 min): unit turn practice against a wall or mirror, ten reps, two-second holds, checking chin, racket head, pointing hand, tucked elbow. The Silk Drop (daily, 5 min): take the racket up and back, then let the head fall on its edge like silk into the slot — no pulling, gravity only — pause a fraction of a second at the bottom, then swing slowly forward and finish over the right shoulder; twenty reps teaches the wrist to stay loose and the chest to load. Quiet Eye + Two-Second Pause (on court, weekly): ten fed balls, hit-freeze-count-turn, each time; four weeks in, expect a genuine 15-20% jump in consistency. Slot + Heel-Toe Shadow Swings (daily, 5 min): no ball, full shadow swing through the compact slot position with heel-to-toe footwork, twenty reps until the footwork becomes automatic. The One-Handed Slice Wall Rally (3x/week, 10 min): as described above. C-Loop vs. Slot Comparison (weekly, 10 min): hit ten balls with each takeback and notice the difference — the C-loop gives more racket head speed but loads the shoulder more, the slot gives less speed but is far more sustainable; past 50, aim for something like 80% slot and 20% or less C-loop. And Mở Mingmen Check (daily, 3 min): takeback position, both hands on the racket, and consciously open the L2-L3 region of your lower back — right hip rotating back, left hip staying forward, lower back expanding; practice the sensation of expansion itself, not the motion, ten reps a day until the lower back learns to load like a spring.

    Cues worth keeping close. Ask the shoulder, not the ego — before deciding whether today's a topspin day. Continental, no exceptions, on every grip check. Hop, then turn, at setup. D-minus-top — the top hand points, it doesn't grip. L-shape load on the takeback. Let the racket head fall like silk on the drop. Pause, then accelerate at the bottom of the takeback. Chest turns, arm follows, on the forward swing. Seventy slow, thirty fast, throughout. Quiet eye, 0.2 seconds, after every contact. Heel-to-toe step on your footwork into the shot. Mở Mingmen as the lower back loads on the takeback. Slot, not C-loop, as your 50+ default takeback choice. Fifteen to twenty degrees of elbow flex at contact, as your 50+ safe arm position. And for the slice specifically: under the ball, open like a hood, soft hands and firm contact, door opens then door closes, and listen for the fwoosh.

    Seven errors that show up constantly at 3.5. Wrong grip — Eastern or Semi-Western, face open at contact — fixed with Continental, V on bevel #2. An arm-pulled takeback, where the racket gets yanked back instead of dropped — fixed with the silk-drop cue and letting gravity actually do the work. A deep left-elbow pull, elbow way behind the back and the shoulder overloaded — fixed by returning to the slot position with the elbow close to the body. A fully straight arm at contact, which shows up later as shoulder pain — fixed with 15-20° of elbow flex. A shallow unit turn, smaller or later than the forehand's — fixed with the hop-then-turn cue and regular mirror checks. Arm-swinging, where the chest stays still and the arm does all the work — fixed with "chest turns, arm follows." And skipping the quiet eye, where the head turns immediately after contact — fixed simply by practicing the one-Mississippi count.

    And the honest shoulder-care conversation. Shoulder impingement from one-handed topspin comes from repeated one-arm load on the dominant side — the answer is using the one-handed slice for anything that would otherwise be a topspin situation, and saving two hands for power. Rotator cuff strain tends to come from the deep left-elbow pull and a too-wide takeback — the slot takeback and heel-to-toe footwork are the fix. Cervical spine load from a deep unit turn — chin fully turned onto the shoulder — gets fixed by letting the shoulders turn while the head stays closer to neutral. Lower back pain on the one-hander usually means excessive hip rotation without a corresponding release, a stiff lumbar spine — the Mở Mingmen practice and genuine hip-shoulder separation are the fix. Interestingly, tennis elbow is actually less of a concern here than on the two-hander, since there's less load on a non-dominant arm that doesn't exist in this stroke. Recovery time matters too — one-handed topspin tends to cost a day or two of soreness, so limit it to once a week; slice, being far gentler, is fine daily. If fear of the backhand side is creating hesitation and late contact, start rebuilding confidence with the slice specifically, since it's the shoulder-friendly entry point. And if you're recovering from a shoulder replacement, most one-handed topspin cues are genuinely off the table — slice only, two hands for power, permanently.

    If there's one rule to take from this whole shoulder conversation, it's this: the one-handed slice is a daily-use shot. The one-handed topspin is a once-a-week luxury. The Wawrinka pull isn't for you at all. Ask the shoulder, not the ego.

 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 1. DEFENSE: "Just get it back deep"
 2. APPROACH: "Slice low, move forward"
 3. RETURN: "Slice is your friend"
 4. RHYTHM: "Slice after topspin rally"

 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 THE 7 DRILLS
 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 1. Wall mirror setup (daily, 3 min)
 2. Silk drop practice (daily, 5 min)
 3. Quiet eye + 2-sec pause (on court, weekly)
 4. Slot + heel-toe shadow (daily, 5 min)
 5. 1HB slice wall rally (3x/week, 10 min)
 6. C-loop vs slot comparison (weekly, 10 min)
 7. Mở Mingmen check (daily, 3 min)

 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 STOP AND RESET IF:
 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 • Shoulder pain during or after (especially on topspin)
 • Elbow pain on the dominant arm (usually a grip-too-tight flag)
 • Cervical spine stiffness after backhand sessions
 • Lower back pain (Mingmen not opening — the spring is stuck)
 • Wrist pain on contact (face angle wrong, or arm too straight)

 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 THE 50+ SHOULDER RULE
 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
 1HB slice = daily-use
 1HB topspin = 1x/week max
 1HB Wawrinka pull = not for you
 Ask the shoulder, not the ego.
